Why Is There a Lack of Irish History in the UK Education System?

An Extended Project Qualification examining the structural, professional, and political factors behind curriculum omissions

Read More

Introduction

In December 2017, a seemingly simple question exposed a profound gap in British public understanding. As Brexit negotiations reached a critical impasse over the Irish border, journalists and commentators expressed surprise that this "suddenly" had become an issue.

British politicians appeared genuinely baffled by Ireland's insistence that no Brexit deal could proceed without guarantees protecting the Good Friday Agreement.

Many British negotiators could benefit from a crash course in the lessons of Irish history... Some former Eton and Oxford students displayed sheer ignorance, and sometimes arrogance, of the realities of Irish history.

This was not merely a diplomatic embarrassment; it revealed a systematic educational failure. How had British students graduated without understanding the history of their nearest neighbour, a relationship spanning eight centuries and shaping both nations profoundly?

The answer lies not in individual failings but in the structure of British education itself. Irish history is largely absent from English, Welsh, and Scottish curricula, relegated to optional status and rarely taught. A 2006 study by history educator Paul Bracey found that only 5.06% of history teachers mentioned Irish dimensions in their curriculum. Recent research by Janssen (2023) analysing history textbooks from 2010-2020 confirms this pattern: the Great Irish Famine receives minimal coverage in English textbooks compared to Irish ones, and when included, is often framed through a narrow, Anglo-centric lens.

The Real-World Consequences

This disparity raises an urgent question: Why is there such a profound lack of Irish history in the UK education system? This is not simply an academic curiosity but a matter with real-world consequences:

  • The Brexit negotiations revealed widespread British ignorance about why a hard Irish border would threaten peace
  • British politicians could not understand why Ireland could not compromise on this issue, or indeed why Ireland is an independent country at all
  • British footballer James McClean faces yearly abuse for refusing to wear a remembrance poppy – a stance rooted in Irish history that most British fans simply do not understand
  • Students with Irish heritage in British schools rarely see their family histories reflected in the curriculum
  • British students graduate without understanding a key chapter of their own nation's past: the British Empire's longest and nearest colonial relationship

The Central Argument

This essay argues that the absence of Irish history from British education results from interconnected structural, professional, and political factors that mutually reinforce one another:

Structural

Curriculum design makes Irish history non-statutory and optional, forcing it to compete with better-resourced topics.

Professional

Teachers lack training and resources in Irish history, leading them to avoid unfamiliar content.

Political

Irish history is "difficult" and potentially controversial, creating reluctance to mandate its teaching.

These three factors create a self-perpetuating cycle: political sensitivity prevents statutory status, which prevents resource development, which prevents teacher training, which justifies continued omission.

Research Methodology

Research Approach: This study employs a multi-method approach combining curriculum analysis, academic literature review, and comparative research to understand why Irish history remains absent from UK education.

  1. Systematic analysis of curriculum documents from the Department for Education (2014) and examination specifications from AQA, Edexcel, OCR, and CCEA
  2. Academic literature review on curriculum diversity, postcolonial education, and teaching "difficult histories"
  3. Textbook analysis using recent studies (Janssen, 2023) that quantify Irish history coverage
  4. Contemporary sources including teacher testimony and media coverage of Brexit-related ignorance
  5. Comparative analysis with Northern Ireland's CCEA curriculum, which mandates substantial Irish history teaching

Understanding why Irish history is absent from British classrooms is not about assigning blame or inducing guilt. Rather, it is about recognizing that curriculum decisions reflect political and cultural choices about what knowledge citizens need.
