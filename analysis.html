Why the Gap Exists

Examining the interconnected structural, professional, and political factors

Understanding the Interconnected Factors

The previous sections have established that Irish history is absent from British curricula, and that comprehensive teaching of these topics is viable. The critical question remains: why does the gap exist?

The answer comes not from a single cause but from three reinforcing factors: structural curriculum design, resource limitations, and sociopolitical sensitivity. The combination of these creates a self-perpetuating cycle of omission.

Structural Factors: How Curriculum Design Creates Hierarchy

The most immediate explanation for Irish history's absence is the structure of UK curriculum requirements themselves. As established earlier, the Department for Education's 2014 National Curriculum designates Irish topics as "non-statutory" – a seemingly technical difference with major practical consequences.

When content is non-statutory, it enters a competitive market where it must contend with mandatory required topics in limited curriculum space. This competition is unequal.

Teachers working under intense accountability pressure prioritize what will be assessed. Optional content, however valuable, invariably loses this competition (Mansfield, 2015).

The Resource Infrastructure Gap

Examination specifications amplify this marginalization. When schools select GCSE and A-level topics, they make decisions based on available resources and examination support. Irish history options exist, but they appear isolated within specifications dominated by topics with much larger support:

  • Nazi Germany
  • The American West
  • Medicine Through Time

These established topics benefit from decades of accumulated resources: textbooks optimized for each specification, exam-question banks, teacher training courses, online resource repositories, and departmental knowledge passed between teachers. Irish history topics lack this infrastructure, making them professionally risky choices.

Curriculum Overcrowding

Curriculum overcrowding compounds this structural disadvantage. The 2014 curriculum specifies extensive content across Key Stages 3 and 4, from the Norman Conquest through twentieth-century world history, all within approximately 50-60 hours of annual teaching time (DfE, 2014).

Mathematics educator Dylan Wiliam observes that England's curriculum is characterized by being "a mile wide and an inch deep," attempting to cover too much content at superficial levels rather than studying fewer topics thoroughly (Wiliam, 2013).

This structural reality forces schools to make exclusionary choices. When time is scarce and content expectations extensive, teachers rationally eliminate non-assessed material.

Geographic Framing

Moreover, the curriculum's geographic framing contributes to Irish marginalization. The 2014 document repeatedly emphasizes "the history of these islands" and "British history," but as Mansfield (2019) argues, this language is consistently interpreted through an Anglocentric lens:

  • Wales appears primarily through English conquest
  • Scotland through union
  • Ireland through Home Rule debates – all framed as sub-plots within England's master narrative

Professional Factors: The Cycle of Avoidance

Even if curriculum structures permitted Irish history teaching, professional factors create practical barriers. Teacher training in England does not require specific historical content knowledge beyond broad chronological awareness.

Training Gap Analysis

Analysis of PGCE (Postgraduate Certificate in Education) history programs at Leicester, York, Sheffield Hallam, Strathclyde, and Bedfordshire Universities reveals a consistent pattern: trainees conduct "subject knowledge audits" identifying their own gaps, which they then address through independent study.

This model assumes that motivated teachers can fill knowledge gaps independently, but crucially, it creates no systematic requirement to address specific historical content, including Irish history.

The Irish Contrast

Country Training Requirements
Ireland The Teaching Council of Ireland requires secondary history teachers to possess 60 ECTS credits (equivalent to one year of full-time study) with mandatory specific coverage of Irish history.
England No comparable requirement. A trainee could complete their degree studying medieval European monasticism, Renaissance Italian city-states, and American Cold War politics, then qualify to teach British history without ever having studied Irish history formally.

Professional Risk Assessment

This training gap creates understandable professional reluctance. Teachers interviewed consistently identify confidence as a barrier to teaching Irish history:

"Irish history is complicated and politically sensitive. Without proper training and clear curriculum requirements, it's safer to teach topics we know."

— Manchester teacher, TES, 2018

These testimonies reveal that the issue is not teacher resistance but rational professional risk assessment. Teachers facing workload pressures, accountability measures, and limited preparation time logically choose familiar content they can teach confidently.

Schools report this dynamic explicitly: when asked why they do not offer Irish history options, departments cite the lack of suitable resources and trained teachers, which publishers cite when explaining why they do not develop Irish history materials (Historical Association, 2019).

Political Factors: Difficult Histories and the Politics of Curriculum

The deepest explanation for Irish history's absence is political. Curriculum decisions are never pedagogically neutral; they reflect values, priorities, and cultural anxieties about national identity.

What Makes Irish History "Difficult"?

Irish history is what educational researchers term "difficult history" – content that:

  • Challenges dominant national narratives
  • Implicates one's own nation in historical injustices
  • Remains politically contested in the present

(Epstein & Peck, 2018)

Teaching British-Irish history comprehensively requires confronting uncomfortable truths about colonial exploitation, policy-induced famine, violent suppression of independence movements, and sustained discrimination in Northern Ireland. These are not distant, safely resolved historical topics but events whose legacies persist in contemporary politics.

The 2010-2015 Curriculum Reforms

The political sensitivity of Irish history manifests in governmental curriculum politics. The 2010-2015 curriculum reforms, overseen by Education Secretary Michael Gove, explicitly promoted a "more patriotic" approach to British history.

Gove criticized what he characterized as "trashing our past" and advocated for curriculum emphasizing British achievements and institutions. His proposed 2013 curriculum draft included specific British "heroes" students should study but mentioned Ireland only peripherally.

The draft provoked fierce criticism from professional historians and teachers, who argued it promoted uncritical celebration over historical analysis (British Politics, 2019). The controversy demonstrates governmental reluctance to mandate content that might complicate triumphalist national narratives.

Contemporary Political Debates

Parliamentary petitions demanding that colonial history become mandatory in the curriculum have generated intense controversy. A 2020 petition calling for mandatory teaching of Britain's colonial past attracted over 270,000 signatures and prompted a Parliamentary debate, where MPs divided sharply on whether schools should teach "the brutal history of the British empire" or whether such teaching constituted inappropriate guilt-inducement (Hansard, 2021).

The "British Values" Contradiction

The 2013 requirement that schools promote "British values" – defined as democracy, rule of law, individual liberty, and mutual respect – further complicates Irish history teaching. As Mansfield (2015) argues, teachers struggle to reconcile promoting positive national values with teaching historical content that challenges those narratives:

  • How does one teach the suppression of Irish democracy through the Penal Laws while simultaneously promoting British democratic values?
  • How does one examine colonial violence while fostering respect for British institutions?

Teachers interviewed by BERA (2016) reported finding the British values requirement "confusing and slightly baffling" when applied to contested historical content, leading many to simply avoid potentially contradictory material.

The Interconnection: A Mutually Reinforcing Cycle

The critical insight is that these three factors do not operate independently but reinforce each other in a self-perpetuating cycle:

Political sensitivity prevents mandates
→
Maintains non-statutory status
→
Discourages resource development
→
Limits teacher training
→
Produces professional avoidance
→
Generates low demand
→
Confirms political judgment to omit

Each factor provides justification for the others' inaction. This interconnection explains why isolated interventions fail:

  • A well-intentioned teacher might wish to include Irish history but lacks resources and training
  • A school might recognize the educational value but faces curriculum time constraints and examination pressures
  • A publisher might consider developing resources but cannot justify investment without curricular demand
  • A government might acknowledge Irish history's importance but defers to teacher professional judgment and curriculum flexibility

The cycle's persistence demonstrates that Irish history's absence is not accidental oversight but systemic outcome. Changing this requires simultaneous intervention across all three factors: political commitment to mandate inclusion, structural curriculum reform to create statutory status, and professional investment in teacher training and resource development.

Northern Ireland provides the proof of concept – when political will exists, structural and professional barriers become soluble rather than insurmountable. What England lacks is not capacity but priority; comprehensive Irish history teaching is pedagogically possible but politically unpalatable.

The question thus transforms from "why can't we teach Irish history?" to "why won't we?" The answer lies in the uncomfortable recognition that curriculum omissions reflect political choices about whose histories matter and which pasts nations are willing to confront.
